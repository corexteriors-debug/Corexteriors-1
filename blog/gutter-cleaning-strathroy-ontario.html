Gutter Cleaning in Strathroy, ON: Why Fall Maintenance Matters

March 15, 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

Strathroy sits at the edge of Middlesex County's agricultural belt, and that combination of established tree cover and open farmland creates a specific challenge for homeowners: eavestroughs that clog faster than they should. Pollen season in Strathroy hits hard in spring, coating everything including gutters. Come fall, the leaf drop from the mature maples and oaks in older Strathroy neighbourhoods finishes the job.

Blocked gutters in Strathroy don't just overflow — they create a chain reaction of damage that quietly adds up. This post covers what to watch for and when it makes sense to call a professional.

Why Strathroy Gutters Clog Faster Than You'd Expect

The typical Strathroy home is surrounded by a mix of deciduous trees — maple, oak, and ash are the most common — plus agricultural fields nearby that generate airborne debris and seed dispersal from spring through early summer. By the time fall leaf drop happens, gutters are already partially packed from months of soft debris accumulation.

The result is what we call a "sandwich clog" — dry organic matter packed between layers of wet leaf sediment. These are harder to flush than a simple leaf blockage and often require hand-clearing before a flush will even work. Running a hose down a blocked Strathroy eavestrough often just backs up and overflows without moving the blockage at all.

The Ice Dam Risk Specific to Strathroy

Southwestern Ontario's freeze-thaw cycles are hard on gutters. Strathroy gets the same temperature swings as London — daytime thaws followed by overnight freezes that refreeze standing water in blocked gutters. When that happens, ice expands inside the eavestrough channel, pulling fasteners away from the fascia and eventually forcing water up under the roof edge.

Ice dams typically show up as damp patches on interior ceilings near exterior walls — easy to dismiss as a minor issue until the insulation is soaked and the drywall starts bubbling. The fix is rarely the gutters alone — it usually involves fascia repair and sometimes soffit replacement. Keeping Strathroy gutters clear going into winter is the cheapest prevention available.

DIY Gutter Cleaning vs. Hiring a Pro in Strathroy

Single-storey homes: If you're comfortable on a ladder, single-storey gutter cleaning is a legitimate DIY job. Scoop out the debris by hand, then flush with a hose from the end closest to the downspout. Check that the downspout discharges water at least two metres from the foundation.

Two-storey and storey-and-a-half homes: This is where we'd strongly recommend calling a professional for any Strathroy home. Extension ladder work on a sloped eave is a fall risk, and the consequences of a ladder accident on a two-storey home are serious. Beyond the safety issue, professionals flush the full system including underground drainage connections — which are common in newer Strathroy subdivisions — and check for sagging sections or fascia rot that aren't visible from the ground.

We also use blowers for dry debris and flush every downspout individually, which takes longer than a garden hose approach but actually clears the system properly.

Recommended Gutter Schedule for Strathroy Homes

  • Late April / Early May: Post-pollen clean. Maple seeds and fresh organic matter from spring growth need to be cleared before summer rain season.
  • Late October / Early November: After the bulk of leaf drop but before the first hard freeze. This is the most critical clean of the year for Strathroy homes.
  • After major windstorms: Any storm with gusts over 60 km/h is worth a ground-level visual check.

If your Strathroy property has significant tree coverage directly over the roofline, consider adding a third clean in late June or early July — seed pods and spring growth tend to create a secondary buildup after the April clean.
